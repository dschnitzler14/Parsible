In the results section, you finally get the results. This is the core of the paper: what the researchers actually found. The results section should describe the findings and their statistical support, without interpretation (that belongs in the Discussion).

This is also the section where presentation choices can strongly influence how convincing the results appear, so it‚Äôs worth reading carefully.

<ul style="margin: 10px 0 0 18px; line-height: 1.45;">
  <li>Results are clearly structured and correspond directly to the stated aims or hypotheses</li>
  <li>Figures and tables are easy to interpret and match what is described in the text</li>
  <li>Sample sizes (<code>n</code>) are clearly stated for each experiment or figure</li>
  <li>Appropriate statistical tests are used and correctly reported</li>
  <li>Effect sizes and confidence intervals are reported alongside p-values</li>
  <li>Raw data points are shown (e.g. scatter plots instead of bar charts, where appropriate)</li>
  <li>Figure legends fully describe what is shown without introducing new methods</li>
</ul>
